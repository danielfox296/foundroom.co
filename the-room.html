What the room is

A room, not a program.

Most communities for founders are built around the company. Deals, tactics, intros, growth. Those things have their place.

Found Room is built around the founder.

Not as a therapeutic project. Not as a self-improvement program. As a recognition that the most consequential variable in any founder's company and life is the founder themselves — and that variable rarely gets honest attention.

The room creates conditions for that attention. Through retreats, peer cohorts, and an ongoing private community, Found Room holds space for the kind of conversations that don't happen in boardrooms, on podcasts, or at conferences.

No performance required. No pitch. No scoreboard.

Three ways to be in the room.

Retreats

Immersive in-person experiences designed to create genuine discontinuity — a break from the usual context, the usual roles, the usual answers. Where the real conversations happen.

Peer Cohorts

Small groups of founders meeting regularly over a defined period. Structured enough to create momentum, open enough to follow what's actually alive in the room.

Community

An ongoing private space — not a Slack firehose, not a networking forum. A room where founders can think out loud, ask real questions, and find people who understand without explanation.

Found Room — the container for the question

Who belongs

Found Room is not for every founder.

It's for founders who have built something real — and are willing to look honestly at themselves as part of what they've built. Pre-exit, post-exit, or continuing to operate. The stage doesn't matter. The willingness does.

The founding members are being selected personally. Not by application form, not by revenue threshold, not by how well-known you are. By whether you're the kind of founder who makes the room better for everyone in it.

If you're reading this and it resonates — that's usually the right signal.

The room is forming.

Found Room is forming its founding member community now. Founding members shape what the room becomes — the formats, the culture, the standards. This is not a waitlist. It's a conversation.
