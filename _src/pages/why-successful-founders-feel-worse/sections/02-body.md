There is a version of this that gets called imposter syndrome. The explanation goes: you don't feel like you deserve what you've built, so you can't enjoy it. That model has enough truth in it to get repeated. It also misses something.

The founders I know who feel worse than they expected after getting to where they wanted to be are not, for the most part, doubting whether they earned it. They know they worked for it. They have watched enough people fail to know that what they built was real. The imposter frame doesn't quite fit.

What tends to fit better is different: they got exactly where they were trying to go, and the arrival didn't produce what they were implicitly trying to produce. The achievement worked. The thing the achievement was supposed to settle didn't settle.

<div class="section-break" markdown="1"></div>

Most founders who build something serious are running, underneath the business, a parallel project. They're building a company and they're also building a case. A case for their own value. For the rightness of their judgment. For the fact that they were worth backing, worth following, worth paying attention to.

This is not unusual. It's probably part of what makes founders good at what they do. The urgency to prove something pushes past the obstacles that stop people who are less motivated by the question.

The issue is that the case never fully closes. You hit the revenue target and the question upgrades: can you scale it? You scale it and the question upgrades: can you exit? You exit and the question should finally be answered, but instead it asks something else, something harder to specify. The proof never arrives in a form that settles the underlying question, because the underlying question was never really about the company.

<div class="section-break" markdown="1"></div>

There's a version of success that was supposed to mean you no longer needed external confirmation. That at some point you'd have enough evidence that the nagging question about your own worth would go quiet. For a lot of founders, the evidence has long since exceeded any reasonable threshold. The question doesn't care.

What tends to happen instead is a fatigue with the proof-seeking itself. You've hit more milestones than you initially imagined. You have the money, the reputation, the respect of people you respect. And still something isn't finished. The effort required to keep feeding the thing has started to feel out of proportion to what it returns.

The flatness that follows a significant achievement is sometimes that: the moment when the return on proof finally drops below the cost of producing it.

<div class="section-break" markdown="1"></div>

This is worth understanding not because there's a fix for it but because the misdiagnosis tends to be expensive. Founders who feel worse than they should after a success tend to assume the problem is insufficient ambition or insufficient gratitude. So they aim higher or try harder to appreciate what they have. Neither works, because neither addresses the actual situation.

The achievement was always pointing somewhere other than the next target. Finding that somewhere is slower work than building a company. It doesn't come with milestones. There's no closing dinner.

<p class="essay-byline">— Daniel Fox</p>
