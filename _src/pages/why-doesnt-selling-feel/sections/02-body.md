<div class="prose reveal" markdown="1">

There is a version of the exit day that most founders have imagined long before it arrives. Not necessarily in detail, but in emotional texture. The sense that something has been completed. That the years of work, uncertainty, and sacrifice have resolved into something permanent and good. That you will feel, in some durable way, that it was worth it.

The closing dinner happens. The wire hits the account. People say things that are kind and probably true. And then, usually within a few days or weeks, the texture shifts.

Not dramatically. The money is real. The deal happened. The company is now someone else's problem. But the feeling that was supposed to come with all of that either doesn't arrive or arrives and leaves faster than expected.

<div class="section-break" markdown="1"></div>

The version of the exit that founders imagine is usually built on a specific implicit premise: that the exit marks the end of uncertainty about whether the thing they were building was going to work, and whether they were the person who could build it.

That premise is right. The exit does answer those questions. The thing worked. You were the person.

What it doesn't account for is that those questions, once answered, don't leave a clean space behind them. They leave something closer to a vacancy. The daily work of building was also the daily work of answering those questions in real time. Revenue moved or it didn't. The team stayed together or it didn't. Every day was a set of small signals coming back about whether you were headed somewhere.

After the close, the signals stop. Not because things are going badly. Because the game is over.

<div class="section-break" markdown="1"></div>

Founders are good at building toward a target. Most of the psychological and operational machinery that drives a successful company is pointed toward the same place: hit the number, close the round, reach the milestone, get to exit. That machinery doesn't stop working when the exit arrives. It just has nowhere to go.

What tends to fill the gap is activity. Advisory roles. Angel investments. A new project that needs attention. The body of a founder finds its way back toward forward motion almost automatically, because forward motion is what the whole organism was built around.

None of this is wrong. Some of it is genuinely good. The problem comes when the new activity is doing the same job the exit was supposed to end: answering the question of whether the founder is heading somewhere, whether the days are pointed at something real. The activity that looks like what's next is often, underneath, still the same old question.

<div class="section-break" markdown="1"></div>

There is also something that doesn't get said at the closing dinner, which is that the company was doing a job for the founder that had nothing to do with the business itself. The meetings, the team, the daily structure, the sense of being needed, the external measure of whether things were going well. All of that was producing something for you beyond revenue. The exit ends the arrangement.

What the business was doing for you is worth understanding. Not because it means you did something wrong, but because the thing that made the exit feel incomplete is probably related to the thing the business was providing that you hadn't fully named.

The founders who move through the post-exit period without losing a year or two to restlessness tend to be the ones who spend some time on that question. Not as a therapeutic exercise. As practical accounting. The business was doing something. Now it isn't. What was that, and where does it come from now?

<p class="essay-byline">— Daniel Fox</p>
