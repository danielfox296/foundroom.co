<div class="prose reveal" markdown="1">

Nobody mentions it at the closing dinner.

The advisors are there. The lawyers are there. The people who helped get it across the line are raising glasses and saying things that are kind and mostly true. The wire is on its way. The years of work have arrived at their intended destination.

And no one in the room says: there's a reasonable chance you're going to feel significantly worse in the next twelve months than you've felt in a long time.

<div class="section-break" markdown="1"></div>

This is not a rare outcome. Studies on founder mental health and exit transitions show that some form of depression, persistent low mood, or disorientation in the first year post-exit is the norm, not the exception. The research borrows from parallel work on athlete retirement, which found the same trajectory: a meaningful dip right after the transition, early stabilization around month five, real improvement somewhere past a year.

The founding story is a story of build-toward. Every action is pointing somewhere. The difficulty of each day is bearable because the direction is clear and the work makes sense. That narrative has a logic and the founder lives inside it for years.

The exit closes the narrative. Not badly. But closed is still closed.

<div class="section-break" markdown="1"></div>

The depression that follows is usually not clinical in the way the word implies acute suffering. It shows up more as a flatness. A diminished return on experiences that used to produce something. A difficulty engaging with things that seemed interesting before. A restlessness that doesn't attach to any particular cause.

The founder often attributes it to the wrong thing. The transition period is confusing. Maybe it's that the next thing hasn't started yet. Maybe it's a health issue. Maybe it's the marriage. Each possible explanation gets some attention, and none of them quite fits, and the flatness continues.

<div class="section-break" markdown="1"></div>

What's actually happening tends to be more structural.

The company was the primary daily source of the things that make life feel like it's working: direction, purpose, social context, external validation, a reason for the hour to matter. The founder built this provisioning structure over years, and it worked, and then the exit ended it in a single transaction.

There is no gradual withdrawal. There is a closing dinner and then there is a morning when the company no longer belongs to you. The provisioning stops at the speed of a wire transfer.

That gap between what the company was providing and what the post-exit life provides is where the depression lives. It's not a character flaw. It's not ingratitude. It's the predictable result of a provisioning structure ending abruptly.

<div class="section-break" markdown="1"></div>

The reason it doesn't get named at the closing dinner is that naming it would require explaining what the company was actually doing for the founder, which is something most founders haven't named to themselves. The business was a business. The psychological provisioning was invisible, or close enough to invisible that it didn't need to be discussed.

But it was there. And when the business ended, the provisioning ended with it. And the exit-to-depression pipeline runs on that gap.

What closes the gap, eventually, is something the closing dinner can't supply: a gradual and honest accounting of what the business was for, and where those things come from now.

<p class="essay-byline">— Daniel Fox</p>
