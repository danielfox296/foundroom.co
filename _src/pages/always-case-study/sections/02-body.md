There was a company called Always. I built it. And it taught me something about identity that I couldn't have learned any other way, which is that the business you build eventually shapes you whether you're paying attention to it or not.

<div class="section-break" markdown="1"></div>

The apparel industry has a particular relationship to identity. You are making something people put on their bodies, something that tells a story about who they are or want to be seen as. That is the explicit transaction. What I didn't fully see at the start was that the same transaction was happening inside the company, between me and the work.

What I was building, underneath the product, was a version of myself I wanted to believe in. The brand had a voice, and the voice was mine, and making the brand successful was making the voice matter. That's a reasonable thing to want. It's also a very specific kind of load to put on a company.

<div class="section-break" markdown="1"></div>

The identity cost isn't visible from inside the building. You're too busy building. The cost shows up on the exit side, or when things go wrong, or in the specific ways you defend certain decisions that turn out to be more about you than about the business.

I defended some things at Always that I knew were not purely business decisions. The brand needed to feel a certain way. The product needed to look a certain way. These were real convictions, and they weren't separate from the fact that the brand was, at some level, me.

<div class="section-break" markdown="1"></div>

The thing I understand now that I didn't understand then is that you can build a better company and a better relationship to it at the same time. The routing of identity through the business isn't the problem. The unconscious routing is the problem. The decisions you make about the company that you think are business decisions but are actually decisions about yourself, those are where the cost accumulates.

When I look back at Always, I see both things clearly. A real company. And a long conversation with myself that the company was the medium for.

I don't think that's unusual. I think it's the thing that nobody names.
